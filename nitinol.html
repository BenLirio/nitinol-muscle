NiTi · Shape-Memory Alloy

Nitinol: the metal that remembers

Bend it, crumple it, tie it in a knot — then warm it up, and it snaps back to the exact shape it was “trained” to remember. Here’s how it works, and how engineers use it as artificial muscle.

❄️ Cold: bend it into any shape…
01 · The basics

One alloy, two personalities

Nitinol is roughly half nickel, half titanium (the name = Ni + Ti + Naval Ordnance Laboratory, where it was discovered in 1959). Its trick comes from having two different crystal structures that it switches between with temperature — no melting, no magic, just atoms shuffling a fraction of a nanometer.

🥶Cold → Martensite

A soft, low-symmetry structure arranged in herringbone “twins”. The twins can flip orientation under stress, so cold nitinol bends easily and stays bent — it feels like soft, dead metal.

🔥Hot → Austenite

A rigid, highly ordered cubic structure. There is only one way the atoms can arrange themselves — and that arrangement encodes the trained shape. Heat the metal and every atom marches back to its assigned position.

20 °C Martensite · soft

🧠Shape memory effect

Below ~As, deformation just flips twins — recoverable up to ~8% strain. Heating above Af erases it all. This is one-way memory: it remembers hot shape only.

🤸Superelasticity

Above Af, bending force itself converts austenite → martensite. Remove the force and it instantly transforms back. This is why nitinol eyeglass frames and heart stents survive being crushed.

04 · Artificial muscle · interactive

Wire in, muscle out

Run electric current through a nitinol wire and it heats itself (Joule heating), transforms to austenite, and contracts ~4–5% — pulling with enormous force, like a tendon. Coil the wire into a spring and the stroke multiplies. Drive the slider below (or pulse it) to flex this robotic bicep.

🦾Just like a real bicep

Muscle can only pull, never push — so does nitinol. Real robots use it in antagonistic pairs (a “bicep” and a “tricep” wire), or against a return spring, exactly like your own arm. Gravity plays the tricep here.

🌡️The catch

Heating is fast (milliseconds), but cooling sets the speed limit — the wire must shed heat to relax. Thin wires, fans, or water cooling make SMA muscles cycle faster. Efficiency is low (<5%), but the simplicity is unbeatable: no motor, no gears, silent.

05 · Why engineers love it

Muscle-grade numbers

~8%
Recoverable strain — ordinary steel manages ~0.5% before permanent damage.
~200 MPa
Actuation stress. Human skeletal muscle: ~0.35 MPa. Per cross-section, nitinol pulls ~500× harder.
No motor
One wire = a complete actuator. Silent, solid-state, works at any scale down to micro-grippers.
−100…+100 °C
Transition temperature is tunable — shifting the Ni:Ti ratio by 0.1% moves it ~10 °C.
