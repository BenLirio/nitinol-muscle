SMA wire is pull-only, exactly like muscle — so the rig uses an antagonistic pair: CH-A flexes the joint, CH-B extends it. Drive both at once (co-contraction) and the joint barely moves but gets stiff — the same trick your arm uses when you brace for impact.

Watch the asymmetry: temperature rockets up under current but decays slowly — the wire can only lose heat to still air. And the phase change lags with hysteresis: contraction starts above As≈68 °C, but on the way down nothing relaxes until the wire falls below Ms≈58 °C. Heating is electric; cooling is patience.

MODULE 02 Specimen Dossier — What Is This Wire MAT REF NiTi-55
  • Nitinol = Ni + Ti + NOL. Near-equiatomic nickel–titanium, discovered by accident at the Naval Ordnance Laboratory, 1959–62 (Buehler & Wang), when a bent demo strip snapped straight over a pipe lighter.
  • It has two crystal structures. Cold: martensite (B19′), a soft, low-symmetry herringbone that deforms easily and stays put. Hot: austenite (B2), a rigid cubic lattice with exactly one allowed arrangement — the “remembered” shape.
  • The transition is diffusionless — atoms shear less than one bond length. No melting. That is why it can cycle millions of times.
  • Transition temperature is tunable: shift the Ni:Ti ratio by 0.1 wt% and the set-point moves ~10 °C. Muscle-grade wire is tuned so body/ambient = relaxed, ~70–90 °C = contracted.

Bare wire contracts only ~4–5% — strong but short. Wind it into a coil and stroke exceeds 100% of actuator length, paid for in force (it acts as a soft spring). Bundle wires in parallel for force instead. Stroke × force is conserved; geometry just spends it differently — like muscle fiber architecture.

Cooling is the speed limit. Heating takes milliseconds; dumping that heat takes seconds. Push past ~1–2 Hz and the wire never falls below Ms between pulses — stroke collapses to a hot quiver. Fixes: thinner wire (more surface per volume), forced air, or water cooling. Toggle the fan and re-sweep the frequency.
