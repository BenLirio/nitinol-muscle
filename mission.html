Principle of Operation

Nitinol has two crystal phases. Cold, it sits in soft herringbone martensite — a wire pre-stretched in this state holds the stretch. Heat it past its transition (here by Joule heating — just run current through it) and the lattice snaps into rigid cubic austenite, hauling the wire back ~4–5% shorter against hundreds of MPa. Drag the gauge needle and watch the lattice — note the heating and cooling paths differ (thermal hysteresis).

Pull-Only System

Like your biceps, a nitinol wire can only pull. So you mount them the way your arm does: in antagonistic pairs. Heat A to flex, heat B to extend — heat both and the joint goes rigid (co-contraction), exactly how you stiffen your wrist.

Stroke Multiplication

A straight wire gives a brutal pull but only ~4.5% stroke. Wind the same wire into a coil or hang it on a lever and the stroke multiplies — at the exact price of force. Work in ≈ work out: you can trade the ratio, never beat it.

⚠ Known Anomalies — read before flight

  • Cooling sets the speed limit. Heating takes milliseconds; shedding that heat takes seconds. Cycle rate is a thermal problem, not an electrical one.
  • Efficiency <5%. It is a heat engine running across a tiny temperature gap — most input energy leaves as warmth, not work.
  • Thermal hysteresis. The contract and relax temperatures differ by ~20–30 °C, so precise position control needs feedback, not just a setpoint.
  • Fatigue. Work it at full 4–5% strain and it degrades in thousands of cycles; keep strain near 2–3% for million-cycle service life.
